Session 1.2
Why We Change: The Case for Value Selling
Feature selling → solution selling → storytelling. Facilitated presentation + group discussion.
IdentityDiscussion
▾
90 mins
For attendees
Before we build new skills, we need to understand why the old approach falls short — and feel it, not just hear it. We'll look at what prospects actually experience in a feature-led demo, explore the difference between feature selling, solution selling, and storytelling, and be honest about where our demos currently sit. This isn't a lecture — you'll be asked to contribute, challenge, and be candid about your own work. The goal is to leave this session genuinely wanting to change.
The core shift: Feature selling shows what the product does. Solution selling explains how it fits. But storytelling lets the prospect see themselves in the Afterward — and that's what creates trust and moves deals. If this session doesn't land emotionally, the week won't produce lasting change.
Purpose
Create genuine pull for change — not compliance. Participants must leave feeling the inadequacy of feature-led demos in their gut, not just understanding it in their head. They must also be psychologically ready to receive honest feedback and open to changing their approach.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Open from the prospect's chair (15 min): Ask the group to switch seats mentally. "Think of the last time you sat through a demo as a buyer. What did it feel like? What were you hoping for that you didn't get?" Take 3–4 responses, write them up. Then ask: "How often do you think your prospects feel the same way?" Let it sit.
  • 2
    The three selling modes (20 min): Three columns on the whiteboard: Feature Selling / Solution Selling / Storytelling. Walk through the same demo moment in all three modes. Ask: which creates the most trust? Which do they currently operate in most of the time? This is the core case for change — a felt experience of the difference, not a lecture.
  • 3
    The business case (15 min): Present the evidence — internal data if you have it, benchmarks if not. Win rate differences, buyer research. Frame as business reality, not philosophy.
  • 4
    Honest self-assessment (20 min): Each person scores their most recent demo privately: (a) Did I understand the real Audience? (b) Did I truly uncover the Annoyance? (c) Did I paint an Afterward? No sharing yet. Then ask: "What did that feel like?" This is where emotional readiness surfaces. Don't rush it.
  • 5
    The feedback contract (20 min): Name it directly: "This week you'll get honest feedback from me and from each other. Let's agree now how we give and receive it." Write the norms on the whiteboard. Leave them up all week.
What Success Looks Like
The room is quieter than it was at the start. People are nodding, not performing agreement. At least one person has said something honest about their own demos. The feedback contract has been named and agreed. If you're getting polite agreement but no tension, slow down and stay in the discomfort of step 4 longer.
Manager tip: Score your own demo honestly in step 4, out loud, before asking them to do theirs privately. This session is the emotional foundation of the whole week. Don't rush the silence.

Session 1.3
Discovery: Audience, Annoyance & Real Requirements
Scorecard debrief · AAA discovery mapping · your real pipeline
Workshop
▾
90 mins
For attendees
This session opens with a debrief of the demo recording you reviewed before the summit — everyone shares their scorecard and together we build a shared picture of what great looks like. From there we go deeper into discovery through the lens of AAA: Audience (who the real stakeholders are and what they care about), Annoyance (the pain actually costing them something), and Afterward (what winning looks like for them). The richer your picture of all three, the more precisely you can build a story that makes them see themselves in the solution.
Technical win connection: You can't get agreement that the solution meets requirements if you never established what the requirements are. The recording debrief surfaces the gap — the rest of this session teaches you how to close it.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Scorecard debrief (25 min): Go dimension by dimension. Ask 2–3 people to share their score and reasoning. Don't force consensus — disagreements are the richest moments. Pay particular attention to Audience & Annoyance and Technical Agreement Close.
  • 2
    "Steal it / change it" (10 min): Each person shares their one thing to steal and one thing they'd do differently. Capture on the whiteboard — reference points for Wednesday's practice.
  • 3
    The AAA discovery map (20 min): Three layers of great pre-demo discovery. Audience: who is the hero, what do they care about, what does a win look like for them personally? Annoyance: not the surface pain but the underlying cost. Afterward: what does life look like when the problem is solved — in concrete, specific terms? Work in pairs on a scenario you provide, mapping all three.
  • 4
    Where to find the story material (15 min): Sources of AAA intel: CRM notes, AE knowledge, prior calls, LinkedIn, support tickets. Most PSC can describe the Annoyance. Very few have clearly articulated the Afterward for this specific prospect. That's the gap.
  • 5
    Individual application (20 min): Each person maps a real upcoming deal — what they know vs. what they still need to find out.
Manager tip: Choose the recording because it has something to teach, not because it's perfect. The debrief is richest when there's genuine disagreement — lean into moments where the group scored differently.

Session 1.4
In-Demo Discovery & the Annoyance Confirmation
Using the Annoyance to anchor the demo — confirming the Afterward as you go
Workshop
▾
60 mins
For attendees
Discovery doesn't stop when you start the demo. The live session is your best opportunity to keep testing your understanding of the Annoyance and confirming that what you're showing connects to the Afterward the prospect described. This session teaches you to use the demo as a two-way story — you narrate the Afterward, they confirm it resonates.
Technical win connection: Every time you confirm a requirement is met during the demo, you're building the case for the technical agreement at the end. Lock small confirmations throughout — don't wait until the final slide.
How to Run It
  • 1
    The Annoyance anchor (15 min): Name the Annoyance → Show the Afterward → Confirm. "You described [the Annoyance]. Here's what that looks like solved. Does that match the world you're trying to get to?" The group tries different versions: Story / Feature / Almost.
  • 2
    Handling a "not quite" (15 min): Getting a "not quite" during the demo is a gift — real-time information. Practise acknowledging it, exploring it, and either adapting or honestly addressing the gap.
  • 3
    Practise in pairs (30 min): Each pair runs a 10-min demo segment using the Name → Show → Confirm pattern. One-word feedback: Natural / Forced / Missed. Debrief together. Rotate once.
Manager tip: The biggest mindset shift is that getting a "not quite" during the demo is better than getting it after the deal is lost. Encourage the team to welcome pushback in practice.

Session 1.5
Demo Craft: Telling the Afterward Story
Build the story from the Annoyance up — not the feature set down
Workshop
▾
75 mins
For attendees
Instead of showing what the product does, or even how it solves the problem, you tell the story of a prospect who was where this person is now — their Annoyance, their journey, their Afterward. This session teaches you to build demos that start from the Afterward and work backwards, so every screen you show is a chapter in a story the prospect can already see themselves inside. You'll restructure one of your own demos using this approach.
The storytelling shift: AAA gives you the structure. Audience = who the hero is. Annoyance = the problem they're stuck in. Afterward = the world they're trying to reach. Your demo is the bridge — every screen should be a scene in that journey, not a feature on a list.
How to Run It
  • 1
    The story structure (20 min): Introduce AAA as a demo narrative frame. Walk through a real customer example mapped to all three. Show how the demo flows Annoyance → Afterward, each screen a scene in that transition. Ask: does your current demo have a hero, a problem, and a resolution? Or just features?
  • 2
    Opening with the story (15 min): "You described [Annoyance]. I want to show you what [Afterward] looks like for a team like yours — and exactly how they got there." Practise in pairs — 60 seconds each. Which version makes the prospect feel like the hero?
  • 3
    Feature → solution → story language (15 min): Three modes side by side. Feature: "This dashboard shows real-time pipeline data." Solution: "This means you can see where deals are stalling." Story: "One of our customers used to lose two deals a quarter because their CRO couldn't see which reps needed coaching until it was too late — this is what changed that." Group rewrites 4–5 feature descriptions into story language.
  • 4
    Rebuild your demo (25 min): Each person maps a real demo to the AAA story structure. What stays? What gets cut? What's missing? Share one structural insight per person.
Manager tip: The most powerful thing a PSC can say is "I had a customer in exactly this situation — here's what they did and what happened." Encourage the team to build a library of 3–5 Afterward stories they can pull into any demo.

Session 1.6
Demo Craft: Engagement & Objections
Keeping the room alive and handling curveballs with confidence
Workshop
▾
75 mins
For attendees
You can have a brilliant story-framed demo and still lose the room. This session covers how to keep prospects engaged throughout and handle objections without losing momentum or credibility. There's also a specific skill for PSC owning the technical win: what to do when a prospect raises a requirement your solution doesn't fully meet — the honest conversation vs. the deflection trap.
Technical win connection: Owning the technical win means being honest about gaps, not papering over them. A PSC who handles a capability gap with honesty and a clear alternative builds more trust — and more deals — than one who deflects.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Engagement techniques (15 min): Signs of a disengaging room and what to do. Micro-questions, pausing on impact moments, using the prospect's language. Contrast with the narrate-every-click trap.
  • 2
    Objection types (10 min): Technical, competitive, scope, requirement gap. Which are genuine blockers vs. signals of engagement?
  • 3
    Acknowledge → Clarify → Bridge (15 min): Group practises live with 3–4 real objections. You model, then the team improves your response.
  • 4
    The honest gap conversation (20 min): Practise the honest response — acknowledge the gap, clarify its importance, offer a path. Roleplay in pairs. Which felt more trustworthy — deflect or honest?
  • 5
    Objection gauntlet (15 min): One presenter, rest of group plays difficult prospects. Rotate through 2–3 people. Celebrate sharp handling.
Manager tip: Collect the team's top real-life objections beforehand. Using live pain points makes the gauntlet feel real and the practice land harder.

Session 2.1
Scenario Briefing & Solo Preparation
Everyone receives the same prospect brief — then prepares independently
Prep
▾
60 mins
For attendees
To start the day you'll receive a detailed prospect brief — the same one for everyone. You'll have 45 minutes of quiet solo time to prepare your demo: map the AAA, structure your story, decide what to show and what to cut, and plan how you'll close for technical agreement. No collaboration during prep — your approach should be entirely your own. That's what makes the group learning that follows so valuable.
The Prospect Brief — What to Include
  • 1
    Company profile: Industry, size, current setup. Use a fictionalised version of a real prospect type your team encounters regularly.
  • 2
    Stakeholders in the room: 2–3 attendees with different roles and priorities — a champion, a sceptic, a decision-maker focused on ROI.
  • 3
    The Annoyance: What they shared on the discovery call — surface pain and underlying cost. Include one thing that's unclear so presenters must make a judgment call.
  • 4
    The Afterward: What the prospect said a win looks like — specific outcomes in a specific timeframe. The ending of the story each presenter needs to build toward.
  • 5
    The constraint: One requirement your solution only partially meets. Everyone must decide whether and how to address it.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Hand out as a printed one-pager at 9:00 AM. Walk through it for 10 minutes — answer clarifying questions.
  • 2
    Quiet solo prep 9:10–9:50 AM. No collaboration. You prep too — you'll go first.
  • 3
    At 9:50 AM: 5-minute check-in. Any last questions? Then move straight into the first demo.
Manager tip: Print the brief as a one-pager — it feels more like a real RFP than a slide. Include just enough ambiguity that people make judgment calls. That's where the learning happens.

Round 1 — Live Demos: First Three Presenters

Practice
▾
~2 hrs
For attendees
Three people present back-to-back to the whole group. When you're in the audience, observe silently and score using your pre-work scorecard. When each demo ends, everyone gives structured feedback — one specific observation anchored to a real moment. Because everyone worked from the same brief, feedback is precise: not "you could have done more discovery" but "here's the Annoyance you named differently to how I would have, and here's why it mattered."
What to look for — score against AAA: Did they open by naming the Audience and Annoyance before showing anything? Did the demo tell a story toward a clear Afterward, or was it a feature walkthrough? Did they use "a customer like you" story language? How did they handle the partial-fit constraint? Did they attempt a technical agreement close?

    Presenter self-reflection (2 mins): What went well? What would you change? Presenter speaks first.
  • 3

  • 4
    Rotate immediately. Next presenter steps up — no gap.
Manager tip: You present first. It models vulnerability, sets the standard, and gives the group a shared reference point. After your demo, self-reflect honestly out loud before they give you feedback.

Session 3.2
Locking the Technical Agreement
How to get the prospect's explicit yes — and what to do with whatever answer you get
IdentityWorkshop
▾
75 mins
For attendees
This is the session that makes the whole week complete. Right now, deals move from demo to sales without the prospect ever explicitly confirming that your solution meets their needs. This session teaches you how to create that moment — how to ask for technical agreement in language rooted in the Afterward story you've been telling, what to do when you get a full yes, a partial yes, or a no, and how to make the handoff to your AE count.
The completion of the mandate: Discovery → story demo → technical agreement. Each step this week has been building to this moment. PSC's job isn't done when the screen share closes. It's done when the prospect has explicitly confirmed the solution can meet their requirements.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Why it doesn't happen (10 min): Ask honestly: "How many of you have explicitly asked a prospect whether the solution meets their requirements — in those words?" Discuss why it feels uncomfortable. Name the blockers out loud — they lose power when named.
  • 2
    Three ways to ask (15 min): The explicit ask: "Based on what we've shown today, do you believe this solution can meet the requirements you described?" The Afterward close: "We started today by talking about [the Annoyance]. What you've seen is how [customer type] got to [the Afterward]. Does what you've seen give you confidence we can get you there too?" The summary close: walk through each Annoyance, confirm which ones the demo addressed, invite the prospect to say where they stand. Group tries all three out loud.
  • 3
    Handling the three responses (20 min): Full yes — capture it clearly and communicate to the AE. Partial yes — surface the gap, name it honestly, decide whether to address now or flag in the handoff. No — treat as valuable intelligence. A "no" in the demo is infinitely better than a "no" three months later. Roleplay one of each.
  • 4
    The handoff that changes everything (15 min): What PSC tells the AE — not "it went well" but: which Annoyances were confirmed addressed, where a gap exists, what the prospect's technical confidence is, and the recommended next step. Practise writing in 3 minutes. Share and compare.
  • 5
    Group discussion (15 min): What needs to change in how you operate to make technical agreement a consistent part of every demo? Capture actions on the whiteboard — some will become Thursday's commitments.
Manager tip: The most important moment is step 1 — the honest conversation about why it doesn't currently happen. Don't rush past it. If the team can name the real reason, you've done more to create change than any framework will.

Session 3.3
Practice: The Technical Agreement Close
End your Wednesday demo with the close you didn't use yesterday
Practice
▾
60 mins
For attendees
One short, focused exercise. You'll pick the technical agreement close that felt most natural from this morning's workshop and use it to close out the final two minutes of Wednesday's demo. Same scenario, same requirements — but now you add the one moment that was missing. Two minutes each, one-word group reactions: Natural / Forced / Incomplete / Confident / Almost. Optional immediate retry.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Setup (5 min): Each person chooses their close version. 2 minutes of quiet prep.
  • 2
    Each person closes (2 mins each): Delivers just the close — the final two minutes. Group observes silently.
  • 3
    One-word group reaction (1 min each): Natural / Forced / Incomplete / Confident / Almost. No elaboration — just the word.
  • 4
    One retry each (2 mins, optional): Anyone who wants to go again immediately gets one more attempt.
  • 5
    Debrief (10 min): Which versions landed most naturally? What's the difference between a close that felt bolted on vs. one that felt earned?
Manager tip: Go first again. Model genuine discomfort with the moment — you give everyone permission to feel that too. Natural by the time they're in front of a real prospect, not natural on the first try.
