Session 1.2
Why We Change: The Case for Value Selling
Feature selling → solution selling → storytelling. Facilitated presentation + group discussion.
IdentityDiscussion
▾
For attendees
Before we build new skills, we need to understand why the old approach falls short — and feel it, not just hear it. We'll look at what prospects actually experience in a feature-led demo, explore the difference between feature selling, solution selling, and storytelling, and be honest about where our demos currently sit. This isn't a lecture. You'll be asked to contribute, challenge, and be candid about your own work. The goal is to leave genuinely wanting to change.
The core shift: Feature selling shows what the product does. Solution selling explains how it fits. But storytelling lets the prospect see themselves in the Afterward — and that's what creates trust and moves deals. If this session doesn't land emotionally, the week won't produce lasting change.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Open from the prospect's chair (15 min): "Think of the last time you sat through a demo as a buyer. What did it feel like? What were you hoping for that you didn't get?" Take 3–4 responses. Then ask: "How often do you think your prospects feel the same way?" Let it sit.
  • 2
    The three selling modes (20 min): Three columns on the whiteboard: Feature Selling / Solution Selling / Storytelling. Walk through the same demo moment in all three modes. Which creates the most trust? Which do they currently operate in most of the time? This is the core case for change — a felt experience of the difference.
  • 3
    Honest self-assessment (15 min): Each person scores their most recent demo privately: (a) Did I understand the real Audience? (b) Did I truly uncover the Annoyance? (c) Did I paint an Afterward? No sharing yet. Then ask: "What did that feel like?" This is where emotional readiness surfaces. Don't rush it.
  • 4
    The feedback contract (10 min): "This week you'll get honest feedback from me and from each other. Let's agree now how we give and receive it." Write the norms on the whiteboard. Leave them up all week.
Manager tip: Score your own demo honestly in step 3, out loud, before asking them to do theirs privately. This session is the emotional foundation of the whole week. Don't rush the silence.

Session 1.3
Pre-Work Feedback: Scorecard Debrief & Discovery
Group scorecard debrief · AAA discovery framework · your real pipeline
Workshop
▾
For attendees
This session opens with a debrief of the demo recording you reviewed before the summit — everyone shares their scorecard and together we build a shared picture of what great looks like. From there we go deeper into discovery through the lens of AAA: Audience (who the real stakeholders are and what they care about), Annoyance (the pain actually costing them something), and Afterward (what winning looks like for them). The richer your picture of all three, the more precisely you can build a story that makes them see themselves in the solution.
Technical win connection: You can't get agreement that the solution meets requirements if you never established what the requirements are. The recording debrief surfaces the gap — the rest of this session teaches you how to close it.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Scorecard debrief (25 min): Go dimension by dimension. Ask 2–3 people to share their score and one sentence of reasoning. Don't force consensus — disagreements are the richest moments. Pay particular attention to Audience & Annoyance and Technical Agreement Close.
  • 2
    "Steal it / change it" (10 min): Each person shares their one thing to steal and one thing they'd do differently. Capture on the whiteboard — reference points for Wednesday's practice.
  • 3
    The AAA discovery map (25 min): Three layers of great pre-demo discovery. Audience: who is the hero, what do they care about? Annoyance: not the surface pain but the underlying cost. Afterward: what does life look like when the problem is solved? Work in pairs on a scenario you provide, mapping all three. Share back.
  • 4
    Where to find the story material (15 min): Sources of AAA intel — CRM notes, AE knowledge, prior calls, LinkedIn, support tickets. Most PSC can describe the Annoyance. Very few have clearly articulated the Afterward for this specific prospect. That's the gap.
Manager tip: Choose the recording because it has something to teach. The debrief is richest when there's genuine disagreement — lean into moments where the group scored differently and ask "what did you see that I didn't?"

Session 1.4
Presentation Skills & Professional Presence
How we show up, how we communicate, and the core consultative skills that define great PSC
Workshop
▾
For attendees
A great demo delivered by someone who looks disengaged, speaks over the prospect, or doesn't actively listen isn't a great demo. This session is about the professional presence and consultative skills that sit underneath everything else we're building this week — how you show up physically and virtually, how you make people feel heard, how you carry yourself, and how the small things (camera on, eye contact, body language, genuine curiosity) determine whether a prospect trusts you enough to share what they really need.
Why this matters for the technical win: Prospects give their real requirements to people they trust. Trust is built through presence, listening, and genuine curiosity — not through polished slides. If you don't have this foundation, the best AAA framework in the world won't save you.
Purpose
Raise the floor on professional presence across the team. Some of these skills feel obvious — but most of us have habits we're not aware of that undermine our credibility. This session names them, practises alternatives, and builds shared standards for how PSC shows up in every prospect interaction.
How to Run It
  • 1
    How we show up — virtual presence (15 min): Start with the basics that people often overlook. Camera on, always — no exceptions in a prospect call. Why it matters: a black box with a name on it is not a relationship. Walk through what "good" looks like: camera at eye level, neutral or professional background, good lighting (light source in front, not behind), dressed as you would for an in-person meeting. Ask the group: what does your current setup communicate to a prospect? Be honest. Then cover mic quality, minimising distractions, and the discipline of not multitasking. If you're half in the meeting, the prospect feels it.
  • 2
    How we show up — in-person presence (10 min): For in-person demos and meetings: what you wear, how you enter a room, where you sit, how you set up. Professional dress signals respect — not formality for its own sake, but the message that this meeting matters to you. Posture, eye contact, and energy in the room are contagious. If you're leaning back with arms crossed, you've already told the prospect how you feel about the meeting. Cover the basics: arrive early, know the room, be the most prepared person there.
  • 3
    Active listening — the hardest skill in the room (20 min): Most people listen in order to respond. Active listening means listening in order to understand. Walk through the difference: the listener who is already forming their answer vs. the listener who is genuinely absorbing what's being said. Practical techniques: note-taking (shows you value what they're saying), mirroring language (using the prospect's own words back to them), pausing before responding (silence is not a problem to fill), and reflecting before redirecting ("So what I'm hearing is… is that right?"). Run a short roleplay: one person shares a problem, the other practises active listening without preparing a response. Debrief: what did it feel like to be genuinely listened to?
  • 4
    Consultative communication — asking better questions (20 min): The difference between a PSC who presents and a PSC who consults is the quality of their questions. Cover three types: open questions that expand ("Tell me more about how that affects your team"), clarifying questions that deepen ("When you say it's slow — what does slow actually mean in terms of time or cost?"), and confirming questions that close loops ("So if I've understood correctly, the priority is X rather than Y — is that right?"). The group practises converting closed or leading questions into consultative ones. Example: "Do you like the reporting?" becomes "What would ideal reporting look like for your team?" Run through 5–6 examples together, the group rewrites each one.
  • 5
    Group self-audit and commitments (10 min): Each person privately identifies one presence habit they know they have that undermines their credibility — camera off, talking over people, not pausing, checking their phone, poor setup. No sharing required. Then each person states one thing they're going to do differently in their next prospect call. Capture on the whiteboard alongside Wednesday's prep notes.
What Success Looks Like
People are a little uncomfortable — because they've identified a real habit they know they should change. The roleplay in step 3 has created at least one moment where someone felt genuinely listened to and named it. The group has agreed on a shared standard for how PSC shows up — not written rules, but a collective understanding of what "we do things properly" looks like for this team.
Manager tip: Model this before you teach it. In the days leading up to the summit, send a note about your own camera setup or share something you've been working on in your own communication style. It signals that these skills aren't just for junior people — they matter at every level. During the session, go first in the self-audit and share your own habit honestly. The team will follow your lead.

Session 1.5
Demo Prep Workshop
What expectations to set, how to handle limited discovery info, and how to structure the Afterward story
Workshop
▾
For attendees
What do you do when you don't have all the discovery you need before a demo? How do you set expectations with the prospect, structure a story-led demo without perfect information, and still aim for technical agreement at the end? This session teaches you the prep disciplines that separate PSC who wing demos from those who own them — including how to deal with limited information in the discovery phase.
The storytelling shift: AAA gives you the structure. Audience = who the hero is. Annoyance = the problem they're stuck in. Afterward = the world they're trying to reach. Your demo is the bridge — every screen you show should be a scene in that journey, not a feature on a list.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Setting expectations (15 min): Walk through how to open a demo when discovery is incomplete. How to name what you know, acknowledge what you don't, and use the demo itself to confirm. "Based on what I know so far, I've structured this around [Annoyance] — I want to check that's right as we go."
  • 2
    The Afterward story structure (20 min): Introduce AAA as a demo narrative frame. Walk through a real customer example mapped to all three. Show how the demo flows Annoyance → Afterward, each screen a scene in that transition. Does your current demo have a hero, a problem, and a resolution? Or just features?
  • 3
    Feature → solution → story language (15 min): Three modes side by side. Feature: "This dashboard shows real-time pipeline data." Solution: "This means you can see where deals are stalling." Story: "One of our customers used to lose two deals a quarter because their CRO couldn't see which reps needed coaching until it was too late — this is what changed that." Group rewrites 4–5 feature descriptions into story language.
  • 4
    Demo opening practice (25 min): Everyone practises a 90-second demo opening in the story frame — confirming what they know about the Annoyance and setting up the Afterward before showing anything. Pairs swap feedback. What made it feel like a story vs. a product tour?
Manager tip: The most powerful thing a PSC can say in a demo is "I had a customer in exactly this situation — here's what they did and what happened." Encourage the team to build a library of 3–5 Afterward stories they can pull into any demo.

Session 2.1
Prospect Brief & Solo Prep
Receive the brief — then prepare your demo independently
Prep
▾
For attendees
You'll receive a detailed prospect brief — the same one for everyone. 45 minutes of quiet solo prep time: map the AAA, structure your story, decide what to show and what to cut, and plan how you'll close for technical agreement. No collaboration during prep — your approach should be entirely your own. That's what makes the group learning that follows so valuable.
The Prospect Brief — What to Include
  • 1
    Company profile: Industry, size, current setup. Fictionalised version of a real prospect type your team encounters regularly.
  • 2
    Stakeholders in the room: 2–3 attendees with different roles — a champion, a sceptic, a decision-maker focused on ROI.
  • 3
    The Annoyance: What they shared on the discovery call — surface pain and underlying cost. Include one thing that's unclear or contradictory so presenters must make a judgment call.
  • 4
    The Afterward: What the prospect said a win looks like — specific outcomes, specific timeframe. The ending of the story each presenter needs to build toward.
  • 5
    The constraint: One requirement your solution only partially meets. Everyone must decide whether and how to address it.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Hand out as a printed one-pager at 9:00 AM. Walk through it for 10 minutes — answer clarifying questions.
  • 2
    Quiet solo prep 9:10–9:50 AM. No collaboration. You prep too — you'll go first.
  • 3
    At 9:50 AM: 5-minute check-in. Any last questions? Then move straight into the first demo.
Manager tip: Print the brief as a one-pager — it feels more like a real RFP than a slide. Include just enough ambiguity that people make judgment calls. That's where the learning happens.

Session 2.2
Live Demos
Full demos to the whole group — silent observation, structured group feedback after each
Practice
▾
For attendees
All six presenters deliver their demo back-to-back to the whole group. When you're in the audience, observe silently and score using your pre-work scorecard. When each demo ends, everyone gives structured feedback — one specific observation anchored to a real moment. Because everyone worked from the same brief, feedback is precise and comparative.
What to look for — score against AAA: Did they open by naming the Audience and Annoyance before showing anything? Did the demo tell a story toward a clear Afterward? Did they use "a customer like you" story language? How did they handle the partial-fit constraint? Did they attempt a technical agreement close?
Structure Per Presenter
  • 1
    Demo (15 mins): Full solution demo, silent observation, everyone scoring on their scorecard.
  • 2
    Self-reflection (2 mins): What went well? What would you change? Presenter speaks first, before the group.
  • 3
    Group feedback (3 mins): Each observer gives one specific observation anchored to a scorecard dimension and a real moment. Keep it tight — quality over quantity. You synthesise: the one thing to take forward.
  • 4
    Rotate immediately. Next presenter steps up — no gap.
Manager tip: You present first. It models vulnerability, sets the standard, and gives the group a shared reference point. After your demo, self-reflect honestly out loud before they give you feedback. With 6 presenters at ~20 min each, this session runs to 2 hours — keep feedback tight.

Session 2.3
Group De-Brief
Identify patterns, areas of struggle, and learn from others — six demos, same brief
Team
▾
For attendees
Now that everyone has presented, we step back and look at the whole picture. Six people, same brief, six different demos. What patterns emerged? Where did almost everyone struggle? How to Run It

    The patterns (15 min): "Looking at all six demos — what did almost everyone do similarly? What did almost no one do?" Map shared strengths vs. shared gaps.     The partial-fit handling (10 min): How did each person handle the constraint? Did anyone maintain the Afterward story around it? Or retreat into feature mode? What does the group now believe is the right approach?

Manager tip: If nobody addressed the partial fit honestly, name it: "Not one of us addressed the constraint directly. What does that tell us?" That discomfort, surfaced without blame, is one of the most valuable moments of the whole week.

Session 2.6
Locking the Technical Agreement
Discovery → story demo → technical agreement. How to get the explicit yes.
IdentityWorkshop
▾
For attendees
This is the session that makes the whole week complete. Right now, deals move from demo to sales without the prospect ever explicitly confirming that your solution meets their needs. This session teaches you how to create that moment — how to ask for technical agreement in language rooted in the Afterward story you've been telling, what to do when you get a full yes, a partial yes, or a no, and how to make the AE handoff count. This matters especially for tomorrow: when you present in front of senior leaders, you should be aiming to close technical agreement from the room.
The completion of the mandate: Discovery → story demo → technical agreement. Each step this week has been building to this moment. PSC's job isn't done when the screen share closes. It's done when the prospect has explicitly confirmed the solution can meet their requirements.
How to Run It
  • 1
    Why it doesn't happen (10 min): Ask honestly: "How many of you have explicitly asked a prospect whether the solution meets their requirements — in those words?" Discuss why it feels uncomfortable. Name the blockers out loud — they lose power when named.
  • 2
    Three ways to ask (15 min): The explicit ask: "Based on what we've shown today, do you believe this solution can meet the requirements you described?" The Afterward close: "We started today by talking about [the Annoyance]. What you've seen is how [customer type] got to [the Afterward]. Does what you've seen give you confidence we can get you there too?" The summary close: walk through each Annoyance confirmed addressed, invite the prospect to say where they stand. Group tries all three out loud.
  • 3
    Handling the three responses (20 min): Full yes — capture and communicate to AE. Partial yes — surface the gap, name it, decide whether to address now or flag in handoff. No — valuable intelligence. Roleplay one of each as a group.
  • 4
    Build it into tomorrow (15 min): Each person identifies exactly where and how they'll attempt technical agreement in their Thursday demo — which version of the close fits their brief, and what they'll do if they get a partial yes. Write it into their demo outline now.
